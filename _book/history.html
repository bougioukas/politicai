2  A short history of AI

Though the vision of intelligent machines has deep roots in ancient philosophy and myth, modern AI emerged as a formal discipline in the mid-20th century. Early milestones include Alan Turing’s foundational inquiries into machine intelligence in the 1950s and the landmark 1956 Dartmouth Conference, where the term “artificial intelligence” was officially coined. From early rule-based systems to today’s sophisticated machine learning algorithms and deep neural networks, AI has rapidly evolved and now influences many aspects of our lives and society.

2.1 Key Landmarks in AI History

  • 400 BCE: The Mechanical Pigeon

In the 4th century BCE, the Greek philosopher and mathematician Archytas of Tarentum reportedly devised a bird-shaped mechanical device known as “The Pigeon,” propelled by a jet of air or steam and said to travel a measurable distance when released from a pivot or wire. The word automaton—from the Greek automaton (“αὐτόματον”), meaning “acting of one’s own will”—was used in antiquity to describe devices that appeared to move without direct human control. From these origins to modern silicon-based systems and drones, a persistent ambition has defined the history of technology: translating biological models and physical laws into engineered systems capable of autonomous or adaptive behavior.

  • 80 BC: The Antikythera Mechanism

In the late 2nd to early 1st century BCE, the Antikythera mechanism — an ancient Greek hand‑powered device often described as the world’s earliest known analog computer — was designed with interlocking bronze gears and dials to calculate and display astronomical information such as the positions of the sun, moon, and planets and to predict celestial events like eclipses.

  • 1897 AD: Gugusse et l’Automate

The 45-second film , made around 1897, marked the first appearance on film of what could be described as a robot. This distinction endeared it to generations of science-fiction fans, even though most knew it only by reputation. Remarkably, the film had likely not been seen by anyone for more than a century. The discovery was made in September 2025 and is now being announced publicly.

  • 1920 AD: Rossum’s Universal Robots

R.U.R. (Rossum’s Universal Robots) is a 1920 science fiction play by the Czech writer Karel Čapek. The play begins in a factory that makes artificial workers from synthetic organic matter. Robots may be mistaken for humans but have no original thoughts. Though most are content to work for humans, eventually a rebellion causes the extinction of the human race.

  • 1928 AD: Gakutensoku: Japan’s First Robot

Thought to be the first robot ever built in Japan, Gakutensoku has a wide range of fluid, dynamic facial expressions and communicates by writing messages with a mechanical pen.

  • 1936 AD: Alan Turing’s concept of a Universal Machine

In 1936, British mathematician and logician Alan Turing, considered the father of computer science, published a groundbreaking paper titled “On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem” (Turing 1937). In this work, Turing introduced what we now call the Turing machine—a simple, abstract mathematical model of a computing device that can read and write symbols on an infinite tape according to a set of rules. He showed that such a machine could, in principle, carry out any calculation that can be described as an algorithm.

  • 1943 AD: McCulloch–Pitts neuron

In 1943, Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts published “A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity”, in which they introduced the concept of the formal neuron, a simplified mathematical model of a biological neuron that represents neural activity as a binary, all-or-none process.
